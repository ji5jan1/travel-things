# Sylvia and Kelly
January 5, 2026

![Plato postcard]()

I almost became a Sylvia Plath scholar. I was serious about that.

When I was in junior high (Secondary 1-3 in HK system), my English was bad—so bad I could never imagine writing this article in English. I failed every English exam in school, and in a school that uses English as the medium of instruction, of course, I also failed other subjects. High school (Secondary 4-6) was when we prepare for the HKDSE (college entrance test, like SAT), and we could pick our electives. In our school, most people take three electives to reach a higher total score in DSE, but as a last-ranking student, I was only allowed to take two, since the school was afraid I couldn’t handle three.

The science electives (Physics, Chemistry, and Biology) were the most popular, and I didn’t have a scientific mind either, so I didn’t consider taking science. Next was Economics and Business, Accounting and Financial Studies (BAFS), but I wasn’t interested in money. Last, we had the humanities: History, Chinese History, Literature in English, Chinese Literature, and Geography. I picked History and Geography at first, and contemplated whether to take Literature in English. At the time, our whole family had no idea what literature was about, but the subject name “Literature in English” was attractive, and my parents encouraged me to take this subject for a chance learn some English. Eventually, I took History and Literature in English.

I had no idea what literary studies were about at all. In the first year, we had a very nice teacher who simplified the poems and short stories for us, and taught us how to read between the lines. My English reading skills suddenly improved, and I enjoyed her lessons. The teacher moved to Singapore after teaching us for one year, and we had a new teacher, Ms. Candice Ng. 

Ms. Ng had two bachelor’s degree from the University of Hong Kong: the first in law, and the second in arts. She was an elite, and was a very strict teacher. Unlike the first teacher, you felt like you were chased by a lion in her class. Her serious aura urged you to copy everything on the blackboard. We hated her in the beginning, because she wasn’t happy about our depth of study and was pushing us toward the harder mode of studying. In the first few months, no one was happy in class. 

I don’t exactly remember when the moment was that we reconciled. But after all, it was her first year teaching—she was learning too. We later found out that she was so much better and really cared about our results (and our future?). Rather than oversimplifying things for us, she explained the symbolism and historical references in the works we studied. That laid a great foundation for my future studies. I remember she once printed an article on Beelzebub to explain the symbol of the pig’s head in Lord of the Flies, so difficult I couldn't understand at the time. I didn’t know why, but I started to like her class and literary studies.

In the summer before we took the DSE exam, she visited Europe, and brought every one of us a postcard from Greece. It was a dark green postcard, with Plato's sculptured head on the left, and two quotes on the right. It said "Starting is half of each endeavor," and "Man is the measure of all things." I had always put up this postcard near my desk, and even brought it with me to the US. Every time when I felt weak and wanted to give up, I look at this postcard, as it would always cheer me up, giving me the energy to continue fighting. I haven't contacted Ms. Ng since graduating (who does that in Hong Kong?), but she will always live in my mind as the teacher who enlightened my path to literary studies.

In the HKDSE Literature in English curriculum, we had to study three Plath poems: “Mushrooms,” “Crossing the Water,” and “Poppies in July.” Plath’s poems had a special force that I still struggle to explain today—one that drew me toward studying her. For the school-based assessment (portfolio), I wrote an essay titled ‘The representation of life and death in Sylvia Plath’s “Poppies in October” (1962), “Edge” (1963) and “Last Words” (1961),’ pointing out how Plath have hinted her suicide through the imagery in these three poems. It wasn’t a well-written essay, but that was the best I could do at that stage.

Sometimes I feel like that decision to improve my English started a journey that I would never imagine. After the DSE exam, I majored in English at university, and did my first master’s degree in literary studies. Whenever there’re chances to pick our own essay topics, I would always write something about Plath. For my first master’s thesis, I wrote an essay, ‘Defending the Aesthetics of The Bell Jar,’ trying to draw readers from the strong emotions in the book, toward a pure appreciation of its artistic value.

After the first master’s degree, I was completely drawn to the studies of old papers in the archives, and thought about pursuing a PhD in English. I thought my career path would be great if I could stay in academia and become a professor. But life is a joke; an incident happened in my family totally messed up my mind and my plans. I managed to file the application, though. The provisional title was ‘Sylvia’s Garden: Flowers in Sylvia Plath’s Poetry,’ which attempted to understand Plath’s poems through the flowers that appeared in them, and to analyze through language of flowers and folklore. I was pretty confident about it, but didn’t realize how messy my mind was, because when I read it later, I found the research proposal was full of incomplete sentences!

Of course, that great PhD plan failed miserably. I then decided to switch paths, and go for something more practical. And fast-forward: here I am, finishing a second master’s degree in publishing—all started from that moment I chose literature. Perhaps literature had chosen me, too, so I can sit here and share this magical experience that was led by Plath. There’s no coincidence; but fate has led us here.
