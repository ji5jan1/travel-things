# Observations

### Book Covers（ブックカバー）
When you checkout in bookstores, the staff would ask if you'd need a book cover for the books you just got. Most books already have a dusk jacket, but this book cover is another extra coat on top. It's a norm (and a free service) that you get an extra book jacket to keep your book cover clean, and also maintain privacy over the book title that you're reading in public. For the bookstores, it's a form of free advertisement, since the paper book covers they provide is usually printed with the bookstore's name.  

Of course, there are the reusable ones. People would buy or make their own leather, fabric, or paper book covers. I saw some of them on [Pinkoi](http://pinkoi.com/search?q=%E6%97%A5%E6%9C%AC%E6%9B%B8%E5%A5%97) and they are beautiful!

## Fiction

### Bunkos（文庫）
![Apothecary Diaries 6](/travel-things/book-observations/japan/umeda/apothecary-diaries.jpeg "Apothecary Diaries #6")  
Look at the size of the fiction books—they are small as pocket books! The Japanese literature books are usually in fixed size (around A6 size). They are stacked tightly on the bookshelves, and are organized based on the authors.

![Fiction shelves](/travel-things/book-observations/japan/umeda/bunko.svg)
The bigger bunkos (imprints) have their own book shelves in bookstores, and each bunko has their exclusive book spine design style, so it's easy to distinguish books from different imprints from the spines!

### Bunko Catalog
![Book Catalog](/travel-things/book-observations/japan/umeda/catalog.svg)
Next to the shelves I saw a mini book hanged—they are the book catalog published by the imprints. They seemed to be produced with a hole on the top right corner, making it easily tied to the shelves. These catalogs are organized by authors, including the book titles and a strap line that attracts the readers. I can only understand the content by the Kanji, but I guess these catalogs are made for fictions.

---

### Where are the English books?
![Fiction in English](/travel-things/book-observations/japan/umeda/english-fiction.jpeg "Fiction in English")
I had a hard time looking for the English shelf in the bookstores, as they are all in the corners. The English fiction books that they import (as I observed) are all paperbacks, and there are not much choice for them. Also the paperback edition they import don't have sprayed edges!

But I saw quite a lot of translated books, and for most of them, they are in hard covers. In Japan there are fewer proficient English speakers, even people working in tourist areas. I guess this is because their own market is strong enough that they don't have to depend on the foreign market, so learning a foreign language is not necessary for them.

### Red Books（赤本）
![Red books](/travel-things/book-observations/japan/umeda/red-books.jpeg "Red books")
The "red books" are the past papers for university entrance exams. Each book represent one major in a university in Japan. What a stressful shelf of books.

### Expo 2025 Osaka
![Expo 2025 Osaka-themed books](/travel-things/book-observations/japan/umeda/expo.jpeg)
In every bookstore, I saw a shelf/ desk near the entrance that's devoted to the Expo-themed books. The magazine-sized ones are the (non)official guidebooks for visitors in Osaka that are visiting the Expo. On the same desk also displays [_やぶれかぶれ青春記・大阪万博奮闘記_](https://bookmeter.com/books/13124823), which seems to be a memorial of the 1970 Expo that was also held in Osaka.
